Video Mapping
& Circuit Bending

I began exploring circuit bending in high school, but it wasn't until university that I refined the practice, thanks to a deeper understanding of electronics and programming. During this time, I was working with Atmel microprocessors and platforms like Arduino, which made the transition to video mapping much smoother. I started experimenting with visuals using Processing to create interactive projections.

As I developed my skills in video mapping, I worked as a freelancer on various projects, collaborating with friends to create immersive visuals for museums and music events. I utilized multiple projectors, often integrating splitter for multi-projection setups. One of my longest-running projects was with the band Futuremen from 2012 to 2021. Initially, I created visuals using analog circuits and circuit-bent devices, such as the Videonics video equalizer, which is well-known for being easy to modify and hack for creative visuals.

In the early days with Futuremen, I relied heavily on analog setups and circuit bending for spontaneous, unpredictable results. However, as the performances grew in scale and required more reliability, we shifted towards digital solutions to ensure stability. Circuit bending, while creatively inspiring, often produced random and imprecise outputs. After some experimentation, we decided to record the best results and integrate them into the live shows as pre-rendered visuals. This allowed us to maintain the aesthetic of analog visuals while ensuring a smoother, more consistent performance.
